================================
What CoppeliaSim Taught Me
================================

Simulating the hexapod before building it compressed months of potential trial-and-error into
a few weeks of rapid iteration. The lessons learned fell into three broad categories.

Mechanical Insights
--------------------

The simulation made it immediately obvious that the initial leg geometry placed the tibia
servo under excessive torque during the stance phase. By visualising the joint-torque readouts
in CoppeliaSim, the femur link length was increased by 15 mm and the nominal stance width
was widened, reducing peak torque to within the rated stall torque of the chosen servo with
an acceptable safety margin.

The simulator also revealed a tendency for the robot to pitch forward during acceleration —
a consequence of the centre of mass being too far forward. This was corrected in the CAD
design by repositioning the battery pack rearward, a change that would have been costly to
make post-fabrication.

Control & Algorithm Insights
------------------------------

Running the tripod gait in simulation exposed a subtle timing issue: when two tripods handed
over within the same control tick, a brief instability appeared in the simulated body pose.
Adding a short dwell period (two control ticks at neutral stance) between tripod transitions
eliminated the artefact and translated directly to smoother hardware behaviour.

The simulation was also invaluable for tuning the IK solver's singularity-avoidance margins.
Edge cases that would have required physical disassembly to reproduce could be induced
repeatably in seconds by manually setting joint angles to boundary values.

Workflow Insights
------------------

Perhaps the most important lesson was the value of the simulation-first workflow itself.
Using the real firmware to drive the virtual robot meant that the software stack was already
well-tested and debugged by the time hardware was available. Integration testing on the
physical robot took only a fraction of the time it would have otherwise required, because the
remaining unknowns were limited to hardware-specific issues (servo backlash, cable routing,
connector reliability) rather than algorithmic ones.

The experience strongly reinforced the value of high-fidelity simulation as a development
tool, not merely a demonstration vehicle.
