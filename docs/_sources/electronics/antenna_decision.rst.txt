=================
Antenna Decision
=================

Selecting the right wireless link for the hexapod required balancing range, latency,
power consumption, and ease of integration with the chosen MCU. Three candidate technologies
were evaluated before a final decision was made.

Candidate Technologies
-----------------------

.. list-table::
   :widths: 20 20 20 20 20
   :header-rows: 1

   * - Technology
     - Range (typical)
     - Latency
     - Current Draw
     - Integration Complexity
   * - nRF24L01+ (2.4 GHz)
     - ~100 m (open air)
     - <1 ms
     - ~11 mA TX
     - Low (SPI)
   * - HC-12 (433 MHz)
     - ~1000 m (open air)
     - ~20 ms
     - ~100 mA TX
     - Low (UART)
   * - ESP8266 (Wi-Fi)
     - ~50 m (indoors)
     - ~5–20 ms
     - ~170 mA TX
     - Medium (AT commands / SDK)

Decision Rationale
-------------------

The **nRF24L01+** module was selected for the following reasons:

- **Latency** — sub-millisecond over-the-air latency is well below the 20 ms control cycle
  period, ensuring that control commands arrive within the same tick they are issued.
- **Current draw** — at ~11 mA transmit current, the module's contribution to the overall
  power budget is negligible compared to the servo load.
- **SPI integration** — the nRF24L01+ uses a standard four-wire SPI bus that the chosen MCU
  supports natively, simplifying firmware development.
- **Proven ecosystem** — the RF24 Arduino library provides a well-tested, low-overhead
  abstraction for packet transmission and acknowledgement.

The HC-12 was ruled out due to its high transmit current and the additional antenna-licensing
considerations associated with 433 MHz operation in some regions. The ESP8266 was eliminated
because Wi-Fi connection setup latency is incompatible with real-time control requirements,
and its higher current draw would complicate the power budget.

Physical Placement
-------------------

The nRF24L01+ module is mounted on the rear-left corner of the body frame, oriented so that
its onboard PCB trace antenna points upward and rearward — away from the servo wiring harness,
which is the primary source of RF interference. A small ferrite bead on the 3.3 V supply line
further reduces conducted noise from the servo power rail reaching the module's VCC pin.

.. note::
   If your deployment environment differs (indoor, long-range outdoor, obstructed), revisit
   the antenna choice. The HC-12 at 433 MHz is a drop-in upgrade for long-range applications
   at the cost of higher current draw.
