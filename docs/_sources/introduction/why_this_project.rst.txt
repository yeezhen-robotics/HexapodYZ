==================
Why This Project?
==================

Motivation
----------

Legged locomotion has long captured the interest of roboticists and biologists alike. Unlike
wheeled or tracked systems, legged robots can traverse unstructured, irregular terrain — steps,
rubble, loose soil — that would immobilise conventional vehicles. The hexapod configuration is
particularly attractive because six legs provide a high degree of fault tolerance: the robot
remains statically stable even if one leg is raised or damaged, since the remaining five can
always form a stable tripod.

This project was motivated by a desire to explore the intersection of mechanical design,
embedded systems, and computational geometry in a single, tangible platform. Building a hexapod
demands proficiency in all three areas simultaneously, making it an ideal vehicle for deep,
integrative learning.

Research into hexapod locomotion spans several decades and draws from biomechanics, control
theory, and mechanical engineering. Early work by :cite:`brooks1989` demonstrated that
reactive, decentralised control architectures could produce surprisingly robust walking
behaviour without any explicit world model. Subsequent studies showed that insects such as
*Carausius morosus* (the stick insect) regulate inter-leg coordination through local sensory
feedback loops rather than a central pattern generator alone :cite:`cruse1990`. These
biological insights directly informed the design of the tripod gait used in this project.
On the computational side, :cite:`siegwart2011` provides a thorough treatment of mobile-robot
kinematics, including the Denavit–Hartenberg convention adopted here for the leg IK solver.
More recently, :cite:`rai2019` demonstrated that simulation-to-real transfer of learned
locomotion policies is feasible for legged robots when the simulation is sufficiently
high-fidelity, motivating the CoppeliaSim-first workflow described later in this documentation.
Further refinements to power-efficient gait selection are discussed in :cite:`garcia2002`,
while :cite:`saranli2001` examines the dynamics of alternating-tripod gaits in the context of
the RHex platform — a hexapod robot that achieved high-speed locomotion on rough terrain.
