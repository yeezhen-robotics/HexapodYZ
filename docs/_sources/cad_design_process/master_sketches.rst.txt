==============================
Learning How to Make Master Sketches
==============================

A **master sketch** (sometimes called a "skeleton sketch") is a top-level 2-D sketch that
encodes the key dimensional constraints of an entire assembly. Child components reference
dimensions from the master sketch rather than having hard-coded numbers. When a critical
dimension changes — say, the body length or the leg attachment pitch — updating a single value
in the master sketch cascades the change through every dependent part automatically.

Why Master Sketches Matter
---------------------------

Without a master sketch, changing a shared dimension such as the servo-mounting hole spacing
requires manually editing every component that references it. On a hexapod with six identical
legs and twelve symmetric mounting interfaces, this is error-prone and time-consuming. The
master-sketch approach converts a tedious global search-and-replace into a single parameter
update.

The master sketch for this project encoded:

- Overall body length and width.
- Leg attachment point positions (pitch circle radius and angular spacing).
- Servo-horn offset distances for each joint.
- Tibia tip contact point positions at nominal stance.

Structure of the Master Sketch
--------------------------------

The master sketch was a single 2-D sketch placed at the assembly origin. It contained:

1. **Construction geometry only** — all lines and arcs were marked as construction geometry
   so they did not generate solid faces. The sketch exists purely as a dimensional backbone.
2. **Named dimensions** — every key measurement was assigned a descriptive name (e.g.
   ``body_length``, ``coxa_length``, ``servo_offset_femur``) so that the parametric
   relationships remained readable.
3. **Symmetry constraints** — left/right and front/rear mirror constraints were applied so
   that only one quadrant of the robot needed to be specified explicitly.

Practical Lessons
------------------

Building the master sketch before modelling any parts proved to be the single most impactful
process decision of the CAD phase. The three design iterations described in the next section
were feasible in a reasonable timeframe precisely because dimension changes propagated
automatically. Without it, each iteration would have required rebuilding several parts from
scratch.
